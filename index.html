Introduction

Between Robert Heinlein's Starship Troopers and Joe Haldeman's The Forever War, similarities are evident—in fact, The Forever War is frequently considered a direct response to Starship Troopers. A significant similarity is present in the importance of the military. Both authors have strong military experiences, which influence their perspectives and thoughts strongly. Heinlein lived through the Cold War and dealt frequently with the idea of nuclear warfare. Haldeman, on the other hand, fought in the Vietnam War, drawing a lot of his military writing from his real experience. While there exists a natural similarity in topic, there are variations in the stances the authors take when considering volunteering for military service, women’s rights and consent, and stance on war.

Military Recruitment

Heinlein advocates a volunteer-based recruitment method, while Haldeman presents a conscripted army, demonstrating the different purposes of war in the two novels.

Consent

An analysis of the way women are treated in the two novels reflects the control given to citizens over their own lives. Heinlein's world gives women much more freedom than Haldeman's.

War Stance

The core stance taken by each of the novels influences the setup of the novels; Heinlein is pro-war, showing the benefits and purpose of war, while Haldeman emphasizes the uselessness and negatives of war.
